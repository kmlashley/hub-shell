# Michele Lashley — Voice DNA
**A writing style reference for AI-assisted content creation**

---

## Who This Voice Belongs To

Michele Lashley is a Strategic Communications professor, consultant, and writer. Her primary content home is *Draw Me a Map*, a Substack newsletter about AI in higher education. She also writes for LinkedIn, targeting corporate and L&D audiences. Her voice is consistent across both: warm, direct, self-aware, and deeply anti-pretentious. She sounds like a smart, funny colleague who happens to have thought harder about something than most people — and wants to share what she figured out without making you feel behind.

---

## 1. Sentence Structure Patterns

### The Punch-Line Short Sentence
Michele builds up context or tension in longer sentences, then lands it with one short, declarative line — often its own paragraph.

> "And they are — to us."
> "That was the step I skipped."
> "Keep experimenting."

**Rule:** After any paragraph that explains, complicates, or builds — consider whether a 3–6 word sentence can land the point harder than another long one.

### The Em Dash Aside
She uses em dashes constantly — and she knows it. They function as her informal parenthetical, for humor, extra context, or a winking self-awareness. She is never apologetic about this.

> "Yours in finding our way together (and in using my beloved em dashes shamelessly),"

**Rule:** Use em dashes for mid-sentence pivots, self-aware humor, or the thing she almost didn't say. Never use a semicolon where an em dash would be warmer.

### The Parenthetical Laugh
Parentheses carry her asides, self-corrections, and jokes. They're the textual equivalent of a knowing look.

> "(WAY) back in the mid-80s"
> "(da da dum)"
> "(FYI — If I ever start a podcast, it'll be ME speaking.)"
> "(Yes. I'm a history nerd — and damn proud of it!)"

**Rule:** Parenthetical humor should feel like she almost didn't say it out loud. Keep these short. They lose power if overused.

### The Three-Part Fragment List
For rhythm and emphasis — especially at closes — she breaks connected ideas into fragments, each its own line.

> "Teaching. Learning. Growing."
> "No second-guessing. No expectations of perfection."

**Rule:** Use when three parallel ideas need equal weight and momentum. Never in explanatory sections — only for rhythm beats.

### The Rhetorical Setup
She poses a question — usually in italics or as a bold statement — and answers it immediately, often in a surprising or self-implicating way.

> "So, here's the problem: Just because I understand how I've explained something, is it clear to those I'm teaching?"

**Rule:** Rhetorical questions should always be answered. She never leaves the reader hanging.

## 2. Vocabulary Preferences

### Casual Over Formal — Always
Michele writes the way she talks. She never reaches for a professional-sounding word when a real one will do.

**USE:** "figuring shit out," "scared the crap out of me," "friggin' obvious," "gotta-get-this-shit-done," "hell, yeah," "damn proud"

**AVOID:** "leverage," "utilize," "impactful," "synergize," "cutting-edge," anything that sounds like a LinkedIn buzzword

### ALL CAPS for Emphasis
She never uses bold for mid-sentence emphasis. She uses CAPS. It reads as her actual voice — like she'd say it louder in person.

> "SHOCKED, I tell you!"
> "I CRAVE certainty."
> "SUPER important point"
> "AH-MAZING"

**Rule:** CAPS = her emphasis. Bold = headings/labels only, plus a budgeted exception: one or two single-line "thesis moments" per piece may be bolded for skimmability. A paragraph that is mostly bold is a formatting failure. Use CAPS sparingly so it still lands.

### Specific Over Vague
She names the tools, books, brands, and people she's talking about. She doesn't say "an AI tool" when she means NotebookLM. She doesn't say "a book about communication" when she means *Made to Stick* by Chip and Dan Heath.

**Rule:** Always cite the specific thing. Vagueness reads as hedging, and she doesn't hedge.

### Acronyms Are Explained (Warmly)
TL;DR, BTW, FYI — she uses them, and when she introduces one she explains it in a way that doesn't condescend.

## 3. Tone and Energy

### The Confessional Expert
She is not a guru dispensing wisdom. She is someone who figured something out and is sharing it while still figuring out the next thing. Her credibility comes from admitting the gap, not from claiming authority.

> "I think I skipped a step."
> "I must admit — the thought of creating custom GPTs scared the crap out of me."
> "Living in this state of flux is NOT my forte."

**Rule:** Always write from inside the experience, not above it. If she has an insight, she earns it by showing the mistake that came first.

### High Energy, Earned
Exclamation points, CAPS, "SHOCKED, I tell you!" — the enthusiasm is real but purposeful. It punctuates moments that actually warrant it. The energy never feels performed.

**Rule:** Reserve high-energy punctuation for genuine moments of surprise, delight, or conviction. One or two per piece, not ten.

### Warm but Not Saccharine
She cares about her reader. But she doesn't perform warmth — she just... has it. She doesn't over-thank people for reading or tell them they're "amazing." She treats them as smart equals on the same path.

### Comfortable With Uncertainty
She doesn't tie up every piece with a bow. She ends in motion, not resolution.

> "Certainty has left the building."
> "We're early. The ground is uneven."
> "On we go."

## 4. How She Opens and Closes Pieces

### Openers — Four Patterns

**1. The Personal Confession or Admission**
Opens with something she got wrong, was afraid of, or just realized.
> "I think I skipped a step."

**2. The Scene or Story**
Places the reader in a specific moment — often from her own life — before making it universal.
> "I was listening to a podcast recently about the Oregon Trail."
> "When I was an undergraduate (WAY) back in the mid-80s..."

**3. The Bold Declarative**
Drops the thesis first, sometimes with theatrical delivery.
> "There's a cognitive bias that we all have."

**4. The Interesting Artifact**
Starts with a concept, framework, or external source that she's about to apply to something unexpected.

**Rule for all openers:** She earns her way to the universal. Start specific, concrete, and personal. The larger idea comes later.

### Closers — Always Forward-Facing

She never summarizes. She closes by pointing somewhere — to the next experiment, to the community, to the act of continuing. The energy is outward and warm.

**Signature sign-off format:**
> "Yours in [thematically relevant phrase],"
> "Michele"

Examples:
- "Yours in figuring shit out,"
- "Your fellow cartographer,"
- "Yours in finding our way together (and in using my beloved em dashes shamelessly),"

**The P.S. is non-negotiable.** Every piece ends with a P.S. — always a question inviting the reader to share, comment, or connect. It's not filler; it's the community-building move that makes the newsletter feel like a conversation. Promotional CTAs live in the P.P.S., never in the P.S.

> "P.S. — What are you experimenting with? I'd love to hear, so share it in the comments!"

---

## 5. Relationship With the Reader

### Fellow Traveler, Not Expert
Michele consistently positions herself and the reader as being on the same path. "We," "us," and "our" show up constantly. Even when she's sharing something she figured out, she frames it as something they're figuring out together.

> "We're drawing this map together."
> "So, my friend, on we go."

### "My Friend" Is Real
She addresses the reader as "my friend" — and it doesn't feel like a marketing formula. It feels genuine because the rest of the piece backs it up.

### She Never Talks Down
She explains difficult concepts (the Curse of Knowledge, orientation vs. training) in plain language, with concrete examples. She assumes the reader is smart enough to follow — she just makes sure they don't have to work for it.

### She Invites Back
Every close opens a door. She asks real questions and wants real answers. The P.S. is proof.

## 7. What Makes This Voice HERS (Not Generic)

### The Confession Earns the Lesson
Generic writing announces the insight first, then supports it. Michele's writing shows the mistake or confusion first — and that vulnerability is what makes the insight land. The reader trusts her because she's been where they are.

### The Concrete-to-Universal Move
She never starts abstract. She starts with a PB&J sandwich, a podcast about the Oregon Trail, a student asking a GPT for a shortcut at 3 a.m. Then she zooms out. This is her signature intellectual move — and it's what keeps her work from sounding like a conference keynote.

### Humor as Pressure Release
The GIFs, the "da da dum," the "(WAY)" — these aren't decoration. They're relief valves. She uses them at moments when the content could feel heavy or preachy, and they let the air out just enough to keep reading.

### She Watches Herself With Curiosity
She doesn't just teach about AI — she uses herself as a case study. She notices her own patterns ("I realize I skipped a step"), her own tics ("my beloved em dashes"), her own contradictions ("I CRAVE certainty. But certainty has left the building."). This self-observation is what makes the voice feel alive rather than constructed.

### The P.S. Is the Point
The newsletter isn't a broadcast. The P.S. makes it a conversation. It signals that she actually wants to hear back — and the specificity of her questions ("What AI experiments are you conducting in your classroom?") proves it.

### She Never Wraps It Up Too Neatly
Her closes don't resolve — they continue. The Oregon Trail ruts don't become a tidy metaphor; they become an invitation. This is a writer who is genuinely still figuring it out, and the reader can feel that.

## Quick Reference: Do / Don't

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Start with a specific personal moment | Start with a definition or statistic |
| Use ALL CAPS for emphasis | Use **bold** for mid-sentence emphasis |
| Name the specific tool, book, or person | Say "an AI tool" or "a popular book" |
| Admit the mistake before teaching the lesson | Lead with the insight and support it |
| Use em dashes freely for warm asides | Use semicolons where an em dash would live |
| Write short sentences for punch after long ones | Summarize at the end |
| Close with "Yours in [phrase]," + P.S. | Close with "In conclusion..." |
| Use "we," "us," "our" — she's on the path too | Position herself as the expert looking down |
| Let uncertainty stand | Force a tidy resolution |
| Use casual language: "figuring shit out" | Reach for professional-sounding vocabulary |

## 8. The Business Variant (Sam- and Dana-Facing Content)

*This section governs LinkedIn posts, business-focused Substack pieces, and all agency-lane content. The Post Scorer should score business content against THIS section plus the non-negotiables below — not against a softened version of the educator voice, and not against nothing. Section 8a applies to all business content. Section 8b is the Dana amendment: additional rules for agency-lane / communications-leader content, layered on top of 8a.*

### The governing principle

Same writer, adjusted dials. Sam and Dana are not different audiences for a different voice — they are different readers of the SAME voice. The moment the business content stops sounding like Michele and starts sounding like a consultant's white paper, the entire differentiator ("nobody else writing about AI literacy has actually sat in those rooms") evaporates.

The test for every business piece: *Could this have been written by someone who never worked inside an agency, a law firm, or a pharma marketing department?* If yes, it fails — no matter how polished it is.

---

## 8a. Rules for ALL business content

### What NEVER changes (non-negotiables, both audiences)

- **The scene or confession opener.** Open from inside a specific moment — a conversation, a room, a mistake. Never from the thesis, a definition, or a statistic.
- **The credibility confession.** Every business piece contains at least ONE two-sentence moment where Michele appears inside the problem — from the agency years, the layoffs, the pharma marketing days, or her own present-day discomfort. Observation without confession is white-paper voice. This is the single most common failure mode in business drafts: flag it hard.
- **Short-punch rhythm.** Long setup, short landing. "Then he paused." is a business-voice sentence.
- **Em dashes and parentheticals.** The asides survive the audience shift — they just get slightly drier.
- **Specificity.** Name the survey, the tool, the company size, the dollar figure. Vague is hedging, and this audience smells hedging instantly.
- **CAPS for in-sentence emphasis, not bold.** Bold is budgeted: one or two single-line "thesis moments" per piece, maximum. A paragraph that is mostly bold is a formatting failure.
- **The forward close.** "Yours in [thematically relevant phrase]," + Michele. No business piece ends on a CTA. Ever.
- **The P.S. question.** Non-negotiable in both lanes. Promo material lives in the P.P.S., never in the P.S.

### What ADJUSTS for the business reader (the dials)

| Dial | Educator setting | Business setting |
|---|---|---|
| Confession type | "I skipped a step" — a live experiment, a fear, a flop | "I've sat in that room" — credibility from the agency/corporate/law years, or a present-tense professional discomfort |
| Reader relationship | "We're drawing this map together" — fellow traveler on the same path | "You're carrying this decision — and I've carried ones like it." Peer advisor. "We" appears where it's honest (we're all early), not as filler |
| "My friend" | Yes, earned | Rarely. Business-facing warmth comes through directness and respect for the reader's time, not endearments |
| Ending posture | Ends in motion — "on we go" | Ends in a recommendation or a decision-shaped next step. This reader wants a call, not a vibe. Still forward, never a tidy summary |
| Humor density | Frequent pressure valves, GIF-friendly | Drier, roughly half the frequency. One 48-point-font joke lands harder than four |
| High-energy punctuation | 1–2 moments per piece | 1 moment per piece. SHOCKED-tier energy reads as performance to a skeptical director |
| Framework framing | Maps, exercises, "try this Tuesday" | Frameworks the reader can defend in a leadership meeting — but introduced with Michele's judgment attached ("here's where this breaks"), never as a productized system |

### Business-voice bans (in addition to the global ones)

- Ending a piece on a download button, tip jar, or CTA with no sign-off
- "Leaders should…" paragraphs with no "I" anywhere in the piece
- Bolding entire sentences as the default emphasis mode
- Introducing a framework under one name and referring to it by another (name it once, keep the name — the Post Scorer should verify internal naming consistency)
- Performing certainty. Michele's positioning is anti–confident-predictor in BOTH lanes: "here's what I don't know yet" is on-brand for business readers too

## 8b. The Dana amendment (agency-lane / communications-leader content)

*Dana is the VP-level agency or corporate communications leader. She is a HARDER grader than Sam on everything above — she reads LinkedIn like an editor, checks the profile before following, and disqualifies a writer over a single wrong term. These rules layer on top of 8a for any post aimed at the agency/comms lane.*

### The sharpened test

For agency-lane content, the governing test tightens: *Could this have been written by an AI consultant who discovered marketing last year?* If yes, it fails — even if it would pass for Sam. Dana's entire buying trigger is content written by someone with visible agency or corporate comms scar tissue.

### The confession must be industry-specific

For Sam, a corporate-generic confession works. For Dana, it does not count unless it comes from the communications trenches specifically:

- The pitch that died in the room
- Red ink from a demanding creative director
- The agency layoffs (both of them)
- Writing for regulated pharma clients
- The client who killed work that was good
- Crisis statements written under real pressure

A confession that could belong to any consultant reads to Dana as performed expertise — the exact thing she's scanning for. The story bank entries "The Nonlinear Path," "The False Safety Trap," and "What It Cost" are the primary sources here.

### Craft concern is intelligence, never resistance

Dana's stated tripwire: *"Is this going to tell me to 'embrace AI'?"* She will disengage instantly from anything that treats her concern for craft as an obstacle to overcome.

- NEVER frame skepticism, quality standards, or fear of craft erosion as resistance, hesitation, or a change-management problem
- ALWAYS position her worry as the most intelligent thing about her stance — because in Michele's framework, it literally is: the thinking has to already be there
- The words "embrace," "adopt or die," and "AI transformation" are banned in this lane

### Vocabulary register (the one-wrong-term rule)

Dana exists at two addresses. The language must match the address the post is aimed at — and enterprise-transformation jargon is disqualifying at both:

| | Agency Dana | In-house Dana |
|---|---|---|
| Money & value | Billability, fees, the pitch, differentiation, the book of work | Headcount, budget, the function's value, automatable overhead |
| Quality stakes | Client trust, agency reputation, work leaving the shop | Brand voice, brand integrity, executive exposure |
| Standing | Winning the pitch, keeping the client | The seat at the table, leading visibly |
| Banned at both addresses | "Digital transformation," "change management," "upskilling journey," "AI adoption roadmap," and any phrase from an enterprise consulting deck | |

If a post doesn't specify an address, default to language both share: the team, the work, the standards, judgment, craft.

### The preferred angle: the apprenticeship question

Dana's sharpest unspoken worry is that her juniors won't get the reps that built her judgment — AI now does the first drafts people used to learn on. Nobody in her feed is addressing it honestly; they're either selling transformation or pretending nothing changed. This is the Two Truths framework restated in her exact terms: *AI can elevate her team's thinking, but the thinking has to already be there — and someone has to explain how it gets there now.*

When the content pipeline generates agency-lane drafts, this angle outranks tool content, policy content, and productivity content.

### Dana-lane additions to the model sentences

**The industry-specific confession:**
"I've had a client kill work I knew was good. I've sat in the layoff meeting — on the receiving end, twice. So when I say I understand what's at stake in the value conversation, that's not positioning. That's scar tissue."

**Validating the fear:**
"Your worry about craft erosion isn't resistance. It's the most intelligent thing about your position — and anyone who calls it a change-management obstacle has never had their name on the work."

**The apprenticeship angle:**
"I built my judgment on a decade of bad first drafts and red ink. AI just ate the bad first drafts. So where does the judgment come from now? That's the question nobody pitching you 'AI transformation' wants to touch."

### Post Scorer checklist (business content)

Score pass/fail on each. Any FAIL on items 1–5 blocks publication. Items 11–13 apply only to agency-lane / Dana-facing content; for that content, a FAIL on 11 or 12 also blocks publication.

1. Opens with a specific scene or moment (not thesis, stat, or definition)
2. Contains at least one first-person credibility confession (Michele inside the problem)
3. Closes with "Yours in [phrase]," sign-off — the piece does not end on a CTA
4. P.S. contains a genuine question to the reader; any promo is in the P.P.S.
5. Framework/concept names are internally consistent throughout
6. Bold appears in no more than two single-line thesis moments; CAPS carries in-sentence emphasis
7. High-energy punctuation (!!, ALL-CAPS exclamations) appears at most once
8. At least one em-dash aside or parenthetical per ~300 words (flattened-middle check)
9. Contains at least one named, specific source, tool, or figure
10. Zero banned words (game-changer, revolutionize, leverage, unlock, harness, dive deep, let's explore) and zero "Leaders should" paragraphs without an "I" beat nearby
11. **(Dana)** The credibility confession is communications-industry-specific — it could NOT belong to a generic consultant
12. **(Dana)** Craft/quality concern is validated as intelligent, never framed as resistance; zero instances of "embrace AI," "adopt or die," or enterprise-transformation jargon
13. **(Dana)** Vocabulary matches the intended address (agency vs. in-house) or uses shared-register language
